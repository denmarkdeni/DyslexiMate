What is dyslexia?

Dyslexia is a learning difference that affects reading, writing, and spelling skills. People with dyslexia may have difficulty recognizing words, decoding sounds, or understanding written text, despite normal intelligence. It’s neurological, not a sign of laziness or low ability, and with support, individuals can thrive academically and creatively.

Frequently Asked Questions

Dyslexia is a common learning difference that primarily affects a person's ability to read, spell, write, and sometimes speak. It's neurological in origin, meaning it stems from how the brain processes language, not from vision problems or intelligence deficits. People with dyslexia typically have normal intelligence and vision but struggle with tasks involving language processing.

A dyslexic-friendly text format uses clear, accessible design to make reading easier for people with dyslexia. This includes sans-serif fonts like Arial or Verdana, larger font sizes, 1.5 line spacing, left-aligned text, and high-contrast colors (like dark text on a light background). It also avoids italics, all caps, and justified text. These changes help reduce visual stress and improve readability.
